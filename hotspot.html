Munnar

Munnar is a town in the Western Ghats mountain range in India’s Kerala state. A hill station and former resort for the British Raj elite, it's surrounded by rolling hills dotted with tea plantations established in the late 19th century. Eravikulam National Park, a habitat for the endangered mountain goat Nilgiri tahr, is home to the Lakkam Waterfalls, hiking trails and 2,695m-tall Anamudi Peak.

history

The region has been inhabited by hunter-gatherer tribals like the Malayarayan and Muthuvan for thousands of years.[7] Tradition states that Colonel Arthur Wellesley, later the Duke of Wellington, was the first British person to pass through Munnar during Tippu Sultan's campaign in Travancore, but this is unsubstantiated. The first survey of the terrain was undertaken by Benjamin Swayne Ward in 1816–1817, who followed the Periyar into the Western Ghats and established a camp at the confluence of three rivers, from which the name of Munnar is derived.[8]

alappuzha

Alappuzha (or Alleppey) is a city on the Laccadive Sea in the southern Indian state of Kerala. It's best known for houseboat cruises along the rustic Kerala backwaters, a network of tranquil canals and lagoons. Alappuzha Beach is the site of the 19th-century Alappuzha Lighthouse. The city's Mullakkal Temple features a traditional design. Punnamada Lake's snake boat races are a well-known annual event.

History

Kuttanad, the rice bowl of Kerala, with its paddy fields, small streams and canals with lush green coconut palms, was well known even from the early periods of the Sangam age.[17] Literary works such as Unnuneeli Sandesam give some insight into the ancient period of this district. Archaeological antiquities, such as the stone inscriptions, historical monuments found in the temples, churches, and rock-cut caves, also emphasise the historic importance of Alappuzha District. Christianity had a foothold in this district, even from the 1st century AD. The church located at Kokkamangalam was one of the seven churches founded by St Thomas,[citation needed] one of the twelve disciples of Jesus Christ. It is generally believed that he landed at Maliankara in Muziris Port, presently known as Cranganore or Kodungallur, in 52 AD and preached Christianity in South India.

Wayanad

Wayanad, nestled in the verdant landscapes of the Western Ghats in the northern part of Kerala, India, is a captivating district renowned for its breathtaking natural beauty and rich cultural heritage. Blessed with rolling hills, dense forests, and meandering rivers, Wayanad offers a serene escape for nature enthusiasts. The district boasts prominent attractions such as the mystical Edakkal Caves, adorned with ancient rock carvings that provide a glimpse into prehistoric times. Soochipara Falls, a three-tiered cascade hidden within lush greenery, beckons with its refreshing allure. For those seeking adventure, Chembra Peak offers a challenging trek rewarded with panoramic vistas. The Banasura Sagar Dam, the largest earthen dam in India, is set against the picturesque Banasura Hills and invites visitors for a tranquil boating experience. Wayanad is also home to wildlife sanctuaries, including the Wayanad Wildlife Sanctuary and Tholpetty Wildlife Sanctuary, where diverse flora and fauna thrive. The district's cultural tapestry is woven with historical gems like Sultan Bathery, which echoes tales of Tipu Sultan's fort and an ancient Jain temple. Pookode Lake, surrounded by emerald landscapes, provides a serene spot for boating. Wayanad's climate, characterized by a pleasant temperature, enhances the allure of its tea, coffee, and spice plantations. The region's unique blend of nature, adventure, and cultural richness makes Wayanad a haven for travelers seeking an immersive and rejuvenating experience.

History

Wayanad, with its enchanting landscapes and rich history, unfolds a tapestry of tales embedded in the very fabric of time. Steeped in antiquity, the region's history reveals traces of early human habitation dating back to the Neolithic Age, as evidenced by the ancient rock carvings found in the Edakkal Caves. Over the centuries, Wayanad became an integral part of the historical and cultural canvas of Kerala. The district's geographical significance was acknowledged in the Sangam age, with Kuttanad, the rice bowl of Kerala, exhibiting its agricultural prowess. The region witnessed the footsteps of St. Thomas, one of the twelve disciples of Jesus Christ, with the church at Kokkamangalam standing as a testament to the early roots of Christianity in Wayanad. The verdant hills and dense forests also served as a natural fortress during various historical periods. Sultan Bathery, originally known as Ganapathivattam, is associated with the military campaigns of Tipu Sultan and the remnants of his fort tell tales of bygone battles. Wayanad's history is further etched in stone with archaeological antiquities, historical monuments, and the cultural practices of indigenous tribal communities, adding layers of depth to the district's timeless allure.

thekkady

Thekkady is a town near Periyar National Park, an important tourist attraction in the Kerala state of India. The name Thekkady is derived from the word "thekku" which means teak. Temperatures are lowest in the months of December–January and highest in the months of April–May.

History

Thekkady, situated in the southern Indian state of Kerala, boasts a rich history intertwined with its lush landscapes. Known for the famed Periyar National Park and Wildlife Sanctuary, Thekkady's history reflects its ecological significance. The region was a dense forest inhabited by indigenous tribes before it caught the attention of the Maharaja of Travancore, who declared it a sanctuary to protect the wildlife. Thekkady gained prominence as a tourist destination when the British transformed it into a game reserve. The Periyar Lake, an artificial reservoir created in 1895, further added to the region's allure. Thekkady's history echoes through the vibrant biodiversity of its national park, with ancient trees and diverse wildlife, making it a haven for nature enthusiasts and conservationists alike.
