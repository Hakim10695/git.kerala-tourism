thiruvananthapuram

Trivandrum, officially known as Thiruvananthapuram, is the capital city of the Indian state of Kerala. Nestled on the southwestern coast of the country, this vibrant city is renowned for its rich cultural heritage, historical significance, and picturesque landscapes. As the political, academic, and administrative hub of Kerala, Trivandrum boasts a unique blend of tradition and modernity. The city is home to several architectural marvels, including the iconic Sree Padmanabhaswamy Temple, a revered Hindu shrine known for its intricate Dravidian architecture. The Napier Museum, built in the 19th century, showcases a remarkable collection of artifacts, sculptures, and paintings, providing visitors with a glimpse into Kerala's artistic heritage. Trivandrum's colonial past is evident in structures like the British-era Kuthira Malika (Puthen Malika) Palace, adorned with exquisite wood carvings and Belgian glass windows. Apart from its historical charm, Trivandrum is surrounded by natural beauty. The city is situated along the Arabian Sea, offering stunning beaches such as Kovalam and Shanghumugham, where visitors can unwind and enjoy breathtaking sunsets. The lush greenery of the Western Ghats envelopes the city, providing opportunities for nature enthusiasts to explore nearby hill stations like Ponmudi and Neyyar Dam.

Among the vast array of tourist spots in Kovalam, it's the beaches that are most sought by the tourists, both local and foreign.

Padmanabhaswamy Temple

The Padmanabhaswamy Temple is a Hindu temple, dedicated to Vishnu, in Thiruvananthapuram, the capital of the state of Kerala, India. It is one of the 108 Divya Desams, the sacred abodes of Vishnu in the Sri Vaishnava tradition. It is widely considered as the world's richest Hindu temple.

veli Lake

The Veli Tourist Village which lies where the Veli Lake meets the Arabian Sea provides for unique boating and picnicking opportunities. Visitors can hire pedal boats or paddleboats as per their convenience. One can also roam the gardens and have a nice picnic or choose to employ the boats for the entire duration of the trip.

kanakakunnu palace

the Kanakakkunnu Palace in Thiruvananthapuram. Located at the heart of the city, it plays a welcoming host to a multitude of cultural events, seminars and programmes. Its proximity to a number of important tourist sites, including the Napier Museum and Trivandrum Zoo, has made it an important part of the daily workings of the city.

kollam

Kollam, situated on the scenic Malabar Coast of Kerala, India, is a city rich in cultural heritage and natural beauty. Known for its historical significance as an ancient trading port, Kollam has played a pivotal role in the maritime history of the region. The city boasts a unique blend of traditional charm and modern developments. One of the prominent attractions is the Ashtamudi Lake, a picturesque backwater stretch that enchants visitors with its serene beauty. The historic Thangassery Lighthouse stands tall as a symbol of Kollam's maritime legacy, offering panoramic views of the Arabian Sea. Kollam is also home to the renowned Jatayu Earth's's Adventure Park, featuring the world's largest bird sculpture and providing a thrilling blend of nature and adventure. The city is adorned with ancient temples, churches, and mosques, reflecting its cultural diversity. Visitors to Kollam can explore the vibrant markets, savor traditional Kerala cuisine, and experience the warmth of the local hospitality. Whether navigating the tranquil backwaters or immersing oneself in the rich cultural tapestry, Kollam invites travelers to discover the unique allure of this coastal gem in "God's Own Country."

pathanamthitta

Nestled in the lush green landscapes of Kerala, Pathanamthitta is a district that captivates visitors with its pristine beauty and cultural richness. Often referred to as the "Pilgrim Capital of Kerala," Pathanamthitta is renowned for its numerous temples, churches, and mosques, drawing pilgrims and tourists alike. The Sabarimala Temple, a sacred Hindu pilgrimage site dedicated to Lord Ayyappa, is a major attraction, attracting millions of devotees during the annual pilgrimage season. The district's diverse topography includes dense forests, glistening rivers, and rolling hills, providing a scenic backdrop for nature enthusiasts. The Perunthenaruvi Waterfall and the Konni Elephant Training Center are among the natural wonders that add to the district's allure. Pathanamthitta also reflects a harmonious coexistence of various religious communities, fostering a cultural mosaic seen in its festivals and traditions. With its tranquil landscapes and religious fervor, Pathanamthitta offers a unique and enriching experience for those seeking a blend of spirituality and natural splendor in the heart of Kerala.

alappuzha

Affectionately nicknamed the 'Venice of the East' by travellers from across the world, Alappuzha is a district of immense natural beauty. Embraced by the Arabian Sea in the west and a network of lakes, lagoons and freshwater rivers criss-crossing it, this backwater country is home toa vibrant animal and avian life. By virtue of its proximity to the sea, the town has always carved out an exclusive place for itself in the maritime history of Kerala. Renowned for its boat races, beaches, marine products and coir industry, the singularity of this land is the region called Kuttanad. A land of lush paddy fields referred to as the 'Rice Bowl of Kerala', it is one of the few places in the world where farming is done below sea level. This once prosperous trading and fishing centre is nowadays a world renowned backwater tourist destination.

Marari Beach, a secluded little known beach destination in the Alappuzha, is located about 11 km from Alappuzha town.The fisherfolk of Mararikulam and their gentle ways of life are some of the attractions here. An evening stroll is one of the best hangouts here.

Alappuzha beach

Alappuzha Beach is a beach in Alappuzha town and a tourist attraction in Kerala, India. Beach has an old pier which extend to sea is over 150 years old. Alappuzha beach host many events annually like Alappuzha beach festival, Sand art festival and many more.

Lighthouse alappuzha

The Alappuzha Lighthouse is situated in the coastal town of Alappuzha, Kerala. It was built in 1862 and is a major tourist attraction. Visitors are allowed between 1500 hours and 1630 hours on every weekday at an admission fee of 20 rupees for Indian citizens and 50 rupees for foreigners.

Punnamada Lake

Punnamada Lake (also called Punnamada Kayal) is a part of the Vembanad Lake, which is the biggest lake in Kerala. Vembanad Lake spans to a number of districts in Kerala. Hence, it is called by different names at different parts of Kerala. When the lake reaches Kuttanad, it is called Punnamada Lake or Punnamada Kayal.

kottayam

Kottayam, nestled in the picturesque landscapes of Kerala, is a district that exudes cultural vibrancy and natural charm. Known as the "Land of Letters, Latex, and Lakes," Kottayam has made significant contributions to the literary and educational heritage of Kerala. The district is home to numerous educational institutions, making it a hub for students and intellectuals. The backwaters of Vembanad Lake grace Kottayam, offering serene boat rides and a tranquil escape into nature. Kottayam is also renowned for its vast expanses of rubber plantations, contributing to the state's thriving rubber industry. The Ettumanoor Mahadeva Temple, known for its architectural splendor, and the St. Mary's Church in Bharananganam, associated with Saint Alphonsa, attract pilgrims and history enthusiasts alike. Kottayam's rich cultural tapestry is woven through its festivals, music, and dance, showcasing the harmonious blend of tradition and modernity. Whether exploring the historic landmarks, enjoying the backwaters, or immersing in the literary ambience, Kottayam invites visitors to experience the unique flavors of Kerala's heartland.

idukki

Idukki, ensconced in the Western Ghats of Kerala, is a district of breathtaking landscapes and natural wonders. Often referred to as the "Spice Garden of Kerala," Idukki is renowned for its lush green hills, dense forests, and extensive spice plantations. The district is home to the Idukki Arch Dam, an engineering marvel and one of the largest arch dams in Asia, offering panoramic views of the surrounding reservoir and hills. The hill station of Munnar, with its rolling tea gardens and cool climate, is a popular tourist destination within Idukki. The district also houses the Periyar National Park, where visitors can embark on wildlife safaris to witness diverse flora and fauna. Idukki is characterized by its picturesque lakes, including the serene Idukki Lake and the mystical Mattupetty Dam. The district's tribal villages, such as Marayoor, add a cultural dimension to the region. Idukki, with its blend of natural beauty and cultural heritage, stands as a testament to Kerala's diverse and captivating landscapes.

thommankuthu waterfall

Thommankuthu Waterfalls is a beautiful spot for anyone visiting Idukki. This seven-step waterfall is among the premier adventure sport locations in the district. The place is famous for its plethora of picnic spots. Trekking aficionados can avail of a local guide to lead them through the woods here. periyar national park

Periyar National Park, also known as Periyar Tiger Reserve, is in the mountainous Western Ghats of Kerala, southern India. This wildlife sanctuary is home to tigers and a significant elephant population, as well as rare lion-tailed macaques, sambar deer, leopards and Indian bison. In the park's north, Periyar Lake is popular for boat rides. Farther north, spice plantations surround the town of Kumily.

ramakkalmedu

Cool, green hills and the fresh mountain air make Ramakkalmedu an enchanting retreat. The place gets its name from the impressions of the feet found on the rock surface here, which is believed to be of Rama, the incarnation of Lord Vishnu, as told in the Indian epic Ramayana.

idukki dam

The Idukki Dam is a double curvature arch dam constructed across the Periyar River in a narrow gorge between two granite hills locally known as Kuravan and Kurathi in Idukki, Kerala, India. At 168.91 metres, it is one of the highest arch dams in Asia. It is constructed and owned by the Kerala State Electricity Board.

ernakulam

Ernakulam, situated on the vibrant Malabar Coast of Kerala, is a bustling urban center that seamlessly combines tradition and modernity. As the commercial capital of the state, Ernakulam is a thriving hub of business, trade, and culture. The district encompasses the city of Kochi, a historically significant port that has been a melting pot of various cultures for centuries. Fort Kochi, with its colonial architecture, ancient churches, and the iconic Chinese fishing nets, reflects the region's rich history of maritime trade. Ernakulam is connected by a network of waterways, including the backwaters of Vembanad Lake, providing scenic views and opportunities for boat rides. The vibrant markets of Broadway and the modern shopping complexes showcase the district's commercial vitality. Ernakulam is also home to cultural landmarks like the Kerala Folklore Museum and the Jawaharlal Nehru International Stadium. With its dynamic urban landscape, historical treasures, and a blend of diverse cultures, Ernakulam offers a unique and captivating experience for residents and visitors alike.

thrissur

Thrissur, often referred to as the "Cultural Capital of Kerala," is a city that pulsates with a rich heritage and vibrant traditions. Nestled in the heart of the state, Thrissur is celebrated for its cultural festivals, art forms, and historical landmarks. The Thrissur Pooram, one of the most spectacular temple festivals in the world, draws visitors with its grandeur, vibrant processions, and traditional music performances. The city is home to the Vadakkunnathan Temple, an ancient Hindu temple with remarkable architecture and religious significance. Thrissur is a hub for traditional arts, including Kathakali, Mohiniyattam, and Theyyam, with institutions like the Kerala Kalamandalam preserving and promoting these art forms. The Athirappilly Falls, often referred to as the "Niagara of India," is a breathtaking natural attraction located near Thrissur. The city's historic landmarks, such as the Shakthan Thampuran Palace and Archaeological Museum, offer glimpses into its royal past. With its unique blend of cultural vibrancy, religious fervor, and natural beauty, Thrissur stands as a captivating destination that encapsulates the essence of Kerala's rich cultural tapestry.

This national park has some rare species of flora and fauna. Silent Valley National Park was explored in 1847 by the botanist Robert Wight.

malappuram

Malappuram, situated in the northern part of Kerala, is a district characterized by its rich cultural heritage, historical significance, and scenic landscapes. Known as the "Land of Hills," Malappuram is adorned with undulating hills, lush greenery, and picturesque valleys. The district is home to the Nilgiri Biosphere Reserve, ensuring a diverse array of flora and fauna. Malappuram has played a pivotal role in the history of Kerala, with remnants of ancient civilizations and archaeological sites scattered across the region. The Thirunavaya Navamukunda Temple, Kottakkunnu Park, and the Kadalundi Bird Sanctuary are among the prominent attractions that draw visitors to this cultural and ecological haven. The district is known for its harmonious blend of various religious communities, exemplified by the various temples, mosques, and churches that coexist peacefully. With its cultural richness, natural beauty, and historical landmarks, Malappuram invites travelers to explore the essence of Kerala's northern region.

kozhikode

Kozhikode, also known as Calicut, is a vibrant city located on the southwest coast of Kerala, India. Steeped in history, Kozhikode has played a significant role in the maritime and trade activities of the region. It was a prominent spice trading center and an essential port during the medieval period, attracting traders from various parts of the world. The city's historical importance is underscored by landmarks like the ancient Kappad Beach, where Portuguese explorer Vasco da Gama first set foot in India in 1498. Kozhikode is renowned for its rich cultural heritage, with attractions such as the Pazhassiraja Archaeological Museum, showcasing artifacts from different periods. The city seamlessly blends its historical past with modernity, offering a mix of bustling markets, educational institutions, and cultural events. Kozhikode Beach, a popular waterfront destination, provides a scenic backdrop for relaxation and recreation. The region is also known for its delectable Malabar cuisine, characterized by a unique blend of flavors and spices. Kozhikode stands as a testament to Kerala's diverse tapestry, welcoming visitors with its warm hospitality, historical charm, and a touch of coastal allure..

wayanad

Wayanad, nestled amidst the Western Ghats in the northern part of Kerala, India, is a captivating destination known for its lush green landscapes, dense forests, and vibrant biodiversity. Wayanad is renowned for its sprawling tea and coffee plantations that carpet the undulating hills, creating a picturesque canvas. kannur

Kannur, nestled along the picturesque Malabar Coast in the northern part of Kerala, India, is a region steeped in history, cultural vibrancy, and natural beauty. Renowned for its pristine beaches, historic forts, and traditional Theyyam performances, Kannur offers a unique blend of coastal allure and rich heritage. The St. Angelo Fort, dating back to the 16th century, stands as a testament to the region's historical significance, providing panoramic views of the Arabian Sea. st angelo fort

Built by the first Portuguese Viceroy in India, Don Francesco de Almeida (1505), St. Angelo’s Fort is among the most historic sites in Kannur. This massive triangular laterite structure is flanked by gigantic bastions that make for an imposing sight. It changed hands between the Dutch and eventually the British, who would remodel and reequip it into their primary military stronghold in Malabar.

Aralam Wildlife Sanctuary is the northernmost wildlife sanctuary of Kerala, southwest India. It is 55 km² in area and located on the western slope of the Western Ghats. It was established in 1984 with its headquarters near Iritty. Aralam Wildlife Sanctuary is situated in the southeast part of Kannur District

The Arakkal Museum is a museum dedicated to the Arakkal family, the only Muslim royal family in Kerala, India. The museum is actually a section of the Arakkalkettu. The durbar hall section of the palace has been converted into a museum by the Government of Kerala.

kasargod

Kasargod, the northernmost district of Kerala, India, is a land of diverse landscapes and cultural richness. Bordered by the Arabian Sea on the west and the Western Ghats on the east, Kasargod is renowned for its enchanting beaches, historic forts, and lush greenery. The district is steeped in history, with the Bekal Fort standing as a testament to its ancient legacy. Perched along the coastline, Bekal Fort offers panoramic views of the Arabian Sea and is a popular destination for both history enthusiasts and nature lovers. Kasargod is also known for its unique Theyyam rituals, a traditional dance form that embodies the spirit of the region's folklore and mythology. The tranquil backwaters of Valiyaparamba and the serene Kappil Beach add to the natural allure of Kasargod, making it a delightful destination for those seeking a blend of history, culture, and scenic beauty.
